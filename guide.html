The one thing everyone should understand

What the colours mean

Every building carries a single readiness colour. It is earned — a building stays red until a fire auditor signs off that its equipment actually works. This is what citizens see.

RED — Not safe

The default. The safety bar below hasn't been met — treat it as not fire ready.

ORANGE — Partly safe

Fire-audit coverage above 60% and every hydrant working.

GREEN — Fire ready

Hydrants, pumps, notifiers and sensors all present and working.

OTP

How everyone logs in

There are no passwords. On the owner app, type your email and tap Send login code — a 6-digit code arrives by email. Type it in to sign in. Being invited is simply having your email added to a building or contractor; the first time you log in, the app finds your role automatically. Citizens don't log in at all.

Who does what
01

Head of Institute

How you get inSigns up themselves with their email.

You own the institute. You add the buildings, invite the people who make each building safe, and watch progress roll in — all from four tabs at the bottom of the screen.

  1. 1

    Register your institute

    After your first login you land on a short form. Type your institute's name, then tap Auto-fill address from my location to fill state, district and locality from GPS (or pick them from the drop-downs). Tap Register institute. You are now the Head of Institute.

  2. 2

    Add your buildings

    On the Buildings tab, tap Add buildings, choose how many you have, and name each one. Each building shows two progress bars — Fire audit and Electrical audit — so you can see at a glance how ready it is.

  3. 3

    Assign a nodal fire officer

    Tap a building to open it, then tap Assign and enter your fire officer's email. That email is the invitation — when they log in, the building is waiting for them. The card turns green once they join.

  4. 4

    Add your contractors

    On the Contractors tab, tap Add contractor, choose Fire or Electrical, and enter their company name and email. They log in with that email and start work.

  5. 5

    Watch citizen concerns

    The Concerns tab collects every issue the public reports on your buildings, with a running count of raised, open, with-contractor and settled.

  6. 6

    Join a parent organisation

    On the Organisation tab you can link your institute under a district or state body: enter their owner's email, pick how much they see, and tap Send link request. They approve from their side.

02

Nodal Fire Officer

How you get inInvited by email on a building. Just log in.

You are responsible for one building. You pin it on the map once, tell the app what's inside it, and then watch every asset and audit for that building.

  1. 1

    Pin the building's location

    Stand at the building and open the Building tab. Tap Capture — the app records your exact GPS position. This is what lets citizens find the building and see its readiness. You only do this once.

  2. 2

    Set up floors and assets

    Enter the number of floors, then for each floor type in the rooms and the count of every fire-safety asset: hydrants, sprinklers, pumps, notifiers and sensors. Tap Save building setup.

  3. 3

    See every asset's health

    The Assets tab shows every asset as a chip — green when working, red when not. New assets start red until a signed fire audit proves they work.

  4. 4

    Track audit progress

    The Audits tab shows how far the fire and electrical audits have got for your building.

  5. 5

    Push concerns to a contractor

    On the Concerns tab you see what the public reported. Tap Push to fire or Push to electrical to send it to the right contractor for repair — then mark it settled when it's fixed.

03

Fire Contractor & Audit Staff

How you get inInvited by email — the contractor by the head, staff by the contractor.

You inspect the fire equipment and put your name to the result. A signed audit is what turns a building green for the public, so signing matters — the app re-checks your identity with a one-time code before it accepts it.

  1. 1

    Open your work

    After login you see your home screen. Tap Go to audits. You can also add your own audit staff with Add staff — they log in with the email you enter and audit on your behalf.

  2. 2

    Pick a building

    Choose the building to inspect. Buildings that the nodal officer hasn't set up yet are greyed out — they need floors and assets first.

  3. 3

    Check every asset

    Tap Start audit session, then for each asset tap the green tick if it works or the red cross if it doesn't. The bar at the top shows how many you've checked.

  4. 4

    Sign with a one-time code

    When you've checked everything, tap Sign audit with OTP. The app emails a fresh 6-digit code to you; enter it and tap Verify & sign. Your signed results become the building's live status — working assets turn green.

04

Electrical Contractor & Staff

How you get inInvited by email by the head of institute.

You record how far the electrical audit has progressed, day by day, for each building — a simple percentage with a note.

  1. 1

    Open a building

    Tap Go to audits and choose a building.

  2. 2

    Record today's progress

    Drag the slider to today's completion percentage, add a short note (what you did, what's pending), and tap Record progress.

  3. 3

    Build a history

    Every entry is kept with its date, so anyone above you can see the electrical audit moving forward over time.

05

Organisation Owner

How you get inSigns up themselves, then approves links from institutes.

You oversee many institutes — a district or state body. Institutes and smaller organisations link under you (with your approval), and you see everything below you rolled up in one dashboard, including a live map.

  1. 1

    Create your organisation

    After your first login, use Create a parent organisation, give it a name, and you land on the Organisation Dashboard.

  2. 2

    Approve incoming links

    When an institute asks to link under you, it appears under Requests waiting for your approval. Tap the green tick to approve. It then shows under Linked below you.

  3. 3

    See the whole subtree

    Under Institutes in your subtree every institute shows its location, building count, citizen-concern tally and audit progress. Tap one to drill into its buildings.

  4. 4

    Open the readiness map

    Tap the map icon in the top bar to see every building you oversee plotted on a map, each pin coloured by its readiness — green, orange or red — so risk stands out instantly.

06

Citizen — FireSafeIndians

How you get inNo account. No name, no phone. Just open the app.

This is the second app, for everyone. It shows you how fire-ready a building is before you enter it, and lets you report a problem anonymously — all it needs is your location.

  1. 1

    See buildings near you

    Allow location when asked. The app lists registered buildings around you, each with a green, orange or red readiness badge. When you arrive at one, a banner tells you where you are and whether it's fire ready.

  2. 2

    Point your camera (AR view)

    Tap AR view and point your phone at a building — a floating readiness badge appears over it, so you can tell which building is which just by looking.

  3. 3

    Raise a concern

    Tap a building, then Raise a concern. You can only report when you're within 40 m of it (the app checks). Describe what you saw and tap Send anonymously.

  4. 4

    Add photo evidence

    Tap Add photo evidence to attach a picture. The app stamps the date, time and GPS coordinates right onto the photo before sending — proof of when and where.

  5. 5

    It reaches the right people

    Your report goes straight to the building's nodal fire officer and head of institute, who push it to a contractor to fix — you stay completely anonymous throughout.

Getting the apps onto a phone: open the download link above on an Android phone, allow “Install unknown apps” for your browser when asked, then open the downloaded file and tap Install. Both apps are available in English and Hindi and follow your phone's language.
